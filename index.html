What it says

In every right triangle — a triangle with one 90° corner — the two shorter sides (the legs) relate to the longest side (the hypotenuse) through an astonishing rule.

a2 + b2 = c2

Square each leg. Add the two squares. The sum equals the square of the hypotenuse — always, for any right triangle anywhere.

Why it matters here

Here, the theorem isn't a destination — it's a tool. You'll use it over and over to find missing lengths inside cones, spheres, and prisms before you can compute their volume. The slant of a cone, the chord of a sphere, the diagonal of a box — all of them are hypotenuses waiting to be found.

Try it yourself

Drag the sliders to change the two legs. Watch the hypotenuse update live, along with the squares on each side.

a² + b² = 25
c = √(a² + b²) = 5.000

The formula

A cube is the simplest solid: six square faces, all the same size. If every edge has length s, its volume is simply:

V = s3

That's it. Multiply the edge by itself three times. Units come in cubed form — cm³, in³, m³.

Where Pythagoras hides

You won't need Pythagoras to find the volume of a cube — but you will need it to find its diagonal. The space diagonal of a cube is √3·s, and that comes from applying the theorem twice: once across the face, once through space.

The formula

A prism is a 2D shape stretched into 3D. Its volume is the area of the base times the depth it extends:

V = B · d

For a triangular prism with a right triangle base of legs a and b, and depth d:

V = ½ · a · b · d

Where Pythagoras hides

If you're told the hypotenuse of the triangular base but not one leg, you need Pythagoras: a² + b² = c² rearranges to give the missing leg. Only then can you find B, and only then V.

The formula

A cylinder is a circle stretched through space — like a prism, but with a circular base instead of a polygon. Its volume is the area of the circular base times the height:

V = π · r2 · h

where r is the radius of the circular base and h is the height.

The connection

The cylinder is the stepping stone between flat shapes and curved solids. Once you know its volume, the cone and sphere follow naturally: a cone is exactly one-third the volume of the cylinder that would enclose it, and a sphere inscribed in a cylinder of the same radius and height 2r takes up two-thirds of it.

The formula

A cone is one-third of the cylinder that would enclose it:

V = ⅓ · π · r2 · h

where r is the radius of the circular base and h is the perpendicular height.

Where Pythagoras is essential

Often, problems give you the slant length ℓ — the distance from the apex to the edge of the base — instead of the height. The radius, height, and slant form a right triangle inside the cone, so:

r2 + h2 = ℓ2

Rearrange to recover the missing height or radius, then plug into the volume formula.

Why one-third?

Take a cone and a cylinder with the same radius and the same height. Fill the cone, empty it into the cylinder, and repeat. It takes exactly three cone-fulls to fill the cylinder — so the cone's volume is ⅓ of the cylinder's.

The formula

Every point on a sphere is the same distance — the radius — from its center. Its volume is:

V = ⁴⁄₃ · π · r3

Where Pythagoras earns its keep

If a sphere sits inscribed inside a shape — say a box — you're often given a chord or a diagonal instead of the radius. Two perpendicular radii plus the chord form a right triangle, so you can solve for r using Pythagoras.

As an example: if two perpendicular radii span a chord of length 6, then r² + r² = 6², giving r = 3√2.

Why four-thirds πr³?

Inscribe a sphere inside a cylinder of the same radius and a height equal to the sphere's diameter (h = 2r). Fill the sphere with water and pour it into the cylinder: the water rises to exactly the ⅔ mark. So the sphere holds ⅔ of the cylinder — and since the cylinder is 2πr³, the sphere is ⁴⁄₃πr³.
